  • Buying: Typically better for 5–10-year-old cars, since leasing usually focuses on new or nearly new cars. Buying allows you to avoid new car depreciation and gives you ownership of a vehicle that’s already taken its biggest value hit.

  • Leasing: Rare for older cars, but some specialty leases or long-term rentals exist.

2. Target Vehicle Age

  • 5 years old: Still modern, relatively low maintenance, warranty may be partially active, and still good resale value.

3. Depreciation Strategy

  • Cars lose the most value in the first 5 years (~50–60%).

  • Buying after 5 years means you avoid this steep initial depreciation, so a 6–10-year-old car often gives the best value for money.

  • Example: A car bought new for £30,000 might be worth £15,000 at 5 years. By 7 years, it may drop to £10,000–£12,000, but maintenance becomes a bigger factor.

4. Maintenance & Reliability

  • 5–7 years old: Often still reliable, low-cost maintenance, and many OEM warranties may still cover major components.

  • 8–10 years old: Expect higher maintenance costs; check service history and common failure points.

  • Strategy: Buy cars known for longevity (Toyota, Honda, some Mercedes, etc.) and check full service records.

5. Ownership Costs

  • Insurance: Usually cheaper than new cars but still varies by model, age, and driver profile.

  • Repairs: Factor in potential replacements like timing belts, brakes, or suspension for 7+ year-old cars.
