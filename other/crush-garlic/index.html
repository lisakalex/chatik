Chefs often crush garlic before cooking because it changes both the flavor and the way it interacts with the dish. Here’s why:

  1. Releases enzymes and compounds

    • Garlic contains alliin (a sulfur compound) and the enzyme alliinase.

    • When garlic is crushed, these mix together to form allicin, which gives garlic its strong aroma and flavor.

    • The more you crush or chop, the stronger and sharper the garlic taste becomes.

  2. Better flavor distribution

    • Crushed garlic spreads more evenly through a dish than whole cloves, giving a stronger, more consistent taste.

  3. Quicker cooking

    • Crushed garlic breaks down faster when heated, so it infuses oil, sauces, or soups more quickly than whole cloves.

  4. Nutritional and health reasons

    • Allicin, formed when garlic is crushed, is linked to health benefits such as antibacterial, antifungal, and potential heart-protective effects.

    • However, allicin is heat-sensitive, so chefs sometimes crush garlic and let it sit a minute before cooking, to allow the allicin to form.

Crushing garlic is simple, and there are a few common methods depending on what tools you have and how fine you want it:

  1. Using a knife (classic method)

    • Place the flat side of a chef’s knife over the garlic clove.

    • Press down firmly with your palm until the clove slightly flattens and the skin cracks.

    • Peel off the skin and chop further if needed.

  2. Using a garlic press

    • Peel the garlic (some presses let you leave the skin on).

    • Place the clove in the press and squeeze.

    • The garlic comes out crushed, ready to use.

  3. Using a mortar and pestle

    • Peel the garlic clove.

    • Place it in the mortar and grind with the pestle until it forms a paste.

    • This method releases more oils, giving a stronger flavor.

  4. Alternative kitchen hacks

    • Wrap cloves in a small bowl or between two sheets of foil and press with a rolling pin.

    • Some use the bottom of a mug or glass to crush a clove on a cutting board.

Tip: Let crushed garlic sit for 5–10 minutes before cooking to let allicin develop fully, which gives stronger flavor and more health benefits.

Yes, you can leave the skin on if you want to, depending on what you’re doing:

  1. If you’re roasting or baking whole cloves

    • Leaving the skin on protects the garlic from burning and makes it easier to squeeze the softened garlic out afterward.

  2. If you’re crushing or mincing for cooking

    • It’s best to remove the skin first.

    • Crushing garlic with the skin on will release less flavor because the papery skin blocks the garlic’s juices and compounds.

  3. Using a garlic press

    • Some garlic presses can press the clove with the skin on, but you’ll usually get slightly less garlic out and may need to scrape it off afterward.

Quick tip: If you want to peel garlic fast, smash it lightly first—then the skin practically falls off.
